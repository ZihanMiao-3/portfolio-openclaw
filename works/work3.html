Managing a 24-Printer Fleet
— Technical Support & Lab Operations

For two academic cycles, I was responsible for the uptime, maintenance, and user-facing support of the School of Mechanical and Manufacturing Engineering's networked 3D printing lab — bridging hardware diagnostics with hands-on student guidance.

In a nutshell: 24 printers were networked via Raspberry Pi for unified control, monitoring and maintenance, with 3DPrinterOS providing real‑time queue visibility, daily preventative checks, and a constant stream of student questions ranging from “how do I slice this?” to “my print is peeling off the bed."
Sep 2024 – Apr 2025 & Sep 2025 – Apr 2026 DCU School of Mechanical & Manufacturing Engineering 24 networked FDM/FFF printers 200+ student users supported

The Setup

A mini server farm for plastic

The lab ran a heterogeneous fleet of Original Prusa MK4 FDM printers — same platform, different quirks. Each machine was paired with a Raspberry Pi running OctoPrint and aggregated into a single 3DPrinterOS queue. Users uploaded STLs through a web portal, and jobs were auto‑scheduled across available machines. My job was to keep the entire pipeline — hardware, software and the humans in between — running smoothly.

1

Hardware Layer

24 Original Prusa MK4 printers with different build volumes, nozzle sizes and use profiles. Each Raspberry Pi handled one‑to‑one printer control, enabling remote monitoring, time‑lapse capture and real‑time error alerts.

2

Software Stack

3DPrinterOS orchestrated the print queue with automatic bed assignment, G‑code streaming and job history tracking. OctoPrint provided per‑machine telemetry — nozzle temperature, bed temperature and print progress at a glance.

3

Support Pipeline

Students submitted support tickets or walked in during lab hours, and I was the first line of support. Issues ranged from slicing settings (Cura profiles, infill, supports) to hardware faults (clogs, bed adhesion, thermistor failures).

Daily Operations

The routines that kept failure rates manageable across two academic cycles.

Shift rhythm: Each morning started with a walk‑through — check active prints, clear completed jobs, scrape beds, reload filament. Then the firefighting began: failed prints, unresponsive Pis, students who accidentally clicked “print all” on a 300‑file batch.
A

Preventative Maintenance

Daily: Bed levelling verification, nozzle wipe, filament tension check, build surface inspection.

Weekly: Belt tension adjustment, lead screw lubrication, thermistor accuracy verification.

Monthly: Full extruder teardown, PTFE tube replacement, Pi SD card health check.

Impact: Recurring faults (clogs, layer shifts, thermal runaway) dropped noticeably after the first full maintenance cycle.

B

Field Troubleshooting

Hardware: Jammed extruders, warped build plates, failed bed leveling probes, power supply failures.

Software: G-code parsing errors, OctoPrint plugin conflicts, 3DPrinterOS connectivity drops.

User side: STL manifold issues, overhangs without supports, unrealistic tolerance expectations.

Approach: Diagnose remotely first via Raspberry Pi telemetry and 3DPrinterOS logs, then escalate to physical intervention when needed.

CAD Design Review

Reviewed SolidWorks part models for printability — wall thickness, overhang angles, fillet radii and tolerance stack‑ups. Often recommended redesigns that significantly reduced support material and print time (typically by 30–40%).

Drawing Verification

Cross‑checked engineering drawings against printed prototypes — verified critical dimensions with callipers and flagged discrepancies between CAD intent and as‑built geometry.

Material Selection

Advised on PLA vs PETG vs ABS based on mechanical and thermal requirements — for example, a wind‑tunnel model needed different behaviour than a functional jig used on a drill press.

SOP Documentation

Wrote standard operating procedures covering printer startup, filament change, bed levelling and emergency stops — standardising training for new lab users and casual staff.

Open Days & Outreach

During faculty open days, I explained how the 3D printing lab works to visiting secondary school students, walking them through a nine‑printer cluster and a table of printed examples before giving a short talk on 3D printing workflows.

Classroom Hardware Mini-Projects

Small 3D-printed interventions that removed everyday friction for lecturers and students in the teaching space.

Touchscreen dock & socket adapter

I designed the entire touchscreen dock, including the 3D-printed enclosure and a removable threaded socket adapter, to fit the existing Crestron panel and the room’s power layout. The original unit was a loose tabletop device with a stiff power cable and no strain relief; the new dock gives it a stable angled base and a side-mounted socket that can be wired from both sides and then fully enclosed, keeping live terminals finger-safe while still easy to service.

Projector–lectern cable hanger

The projector-to-lectern data cable used to hang loosely across desks and chair backs, creating clutter and trip risks. I designed a wall-mounted hanger that parks the cable at eye level near the connection point, so lecturers can grab it quickly without dragging it across the room and the cable jacket sees much less abuse.

Curved projector screen bracket

The original projector screen end cap had a sharp corner that could catch on tables or chair backs when the screen was raised, risking tears in the fabric. I designed a curved 3D-printed bracket that snaps over the end cap so that, if it hits furniture, it slides rather than catches, protecting both the screen and the room layout.

3D-printed speaker enclosure

Outside the lab, I designed and printed a compact speaker enclosure, including the mounting pattern for the driver, amplifier board and crossover. The project combined CAD modelling for acoustics and manufacturability with practical wiring, soldering and assembly, and it helped me think about vibration, clearances and serviceability in small enclosures.

Reflection

"Running a 24-printer lab taught me something no classroom can: how to triage under pressure. When six prints fail overnight and a student's capstone deadline is tomorrow, you don't have time for textbook diagnostics. You learn the failure modes — a specific scraping sound on layer 3, that one printer whose bed always drifts after 20 hours — and you build mental checklists that make you faster every week. That kind of systematic troubleshooting carries directly into field service engineering."

Two things I'd do differently: I'd build a shared dashboard that consolidates all 24 printer telemetry into a single view — too many tabs open every morning. And I'd set up automated filament runout notifications via Telegram or Slack instead of relying on walk-by checks.

What stuck: The confidence to walk into a room of 24 machines and know, within 30 seconds, which one is about to fail. That pattern recognition is hard to teach and harder to forget.
