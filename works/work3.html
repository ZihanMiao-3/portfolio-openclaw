Managing a 24-Printer Fleet
— Technical Support & Lab Operations

For two academic cycles, I was responsible for the uptime, maintenance, and user-facing support of the School of Mechanical and Manufacturing Engineering's networked 3D printing lab — bridging hardware diagnostics with hands-on student guidance.

In a nutshell: 24 printers, one Raspberry Pi backbone, real-time queue monitoring via 3DPrinterOS, daily preventative maintenance — and a steady stream of students who needed everything from "how do I slice this" to "my print is peeling off the bed."
Sep 2024 – Apr 2025 & Sep 2025 – Apr 2026 DCU School of Mechanical & Manufacturing Engineering 24 networked FDM/FFF printers 200+ student users supported

The Setup

A mini server farm for plastic

The lab ran a heterogeneous fleet of FDM/FFF printers — different models, different quirks. Every machine was connected through a Raspberry Pi running OctoPrint, aggregated under a single 3DPrinterOS queue. Users uploaded STLs through a web portal, and prints were scheduled automatically across available machines. My job was to keep the whole pipeline — hardware, software, and the humans in between — running smoothly.

1

Hardware Layer

24 printers with diverse build volumes and nozzle configurations. Each Pi handled one-to-one printer control, enabling remote monitoring, time-lapse capture, and real-time error alerts.

2

Software Stack

3DPrinterOS orchestrated the print queue with automatic bed assignment, g-code streaming, and job history. OctoPrint provided per-machine telemetry — nozzle temp, bed temp, print progress at a glance.

3

Support Pipeline

Students submitted support tickets or walked in during lab hours. Issues ranged from slicing settings (Cura profiles, infill, supports) to hardware faults (clogs, bed adhesion, thermistor failures).

Daily Operations

Routine that kept failure rates manageable across two semesters.

Fleet overview photo — coming soon
Shift rhythm: Each morning started with a walk-through — check active prints, clear completed jobs, scrape beds, reload filament. Then the firefighting began: failed prints, unresponsive Pis, students who accidentally clicked "print all" on a 300-file batch.
A

Preventative Maintenance

Daily: Bed levelling verification, nozzle wipe, filament tension check, build surface inspection.

Weekly: Belt tension adjustment, lead screw lubrication, thermistor accuracy verification.

Monthly: Full extruder teardown, PTFE tube replacement, Pi SD card health check.

Impact: Recurring faults (clogs, layer shifts, thermal runaway) dropped noticeably after the first full maintenance cycle.

B

Field Troubleshooting

Hardware: Jammed extruders, warped build plates, failed bed leveling probes, power supply failures.

Software: G-code parsing errors, OctoPrint plugin conflicts, 3DPrinterOS connectivity drops.

User side: STL manifold issues, overhangs without supports, unrealistic tolerance expectations.

Approach: Diagnose remotely first via Pi telemetry, escalate to physical intervention when needed.

Capstone & Coursework Support

Beyond lab operations, I advised students on physical prototype builds throughout the academic year.

CAD Design Review

Reviewed SolidWorks part models for printability — wall thickness, overhang angles, fillet radii. Often recommended redesigns that reduced support material and print time by 30-40%.

Drawing Verification

Cross-checked engineering drawings against printed prototypes — verified critical dimensions with callipers and flagged discrepancies between CAD and as-built geometry.

Material Selection

Advised on PLA vs PETG vs ABS based on mechanical requirements — a wind-tunnel model needed different material behaviour than a functional jig used on a drill press.

SOP Documentation

Wrote standard operating procedures covering printer startup, filament change, bed levelling, and emergency stop — standardising training for new lab users and casual staff.

Reflection

"Running a 24-printer lab taught me something no classroom can: how to triage under pressure. When six prints fail overnight and a student's capstone deadline is tomorrow, you don't have time for textbook diagnostics. You learn the failure modes — a specific scraping sound on layer 3, that one printer whose bed always drifts after 20 hours — and you build mental checklists that make you faster every week. That kind of systematic troubleshooting carries directly into field service engineering."

Two things I'd do differently: I'd build a shared dashboard that consolidates all 24 printer telemetry into a single view — too many tabs open every morning. And I'd set up automated filament runout notifications via Telegram or Slack instead of relying on walk-by checks.

What stuck: The confidence to walk into a room of 24 machines and know, within 30 seconds, which one is about to fail. That pattern recognition is hard to teach and harder to forget.
